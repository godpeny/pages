# Determinants
## Introduction to Determinants
### The Determinant of a Matrix
For $n>1$,
$$
\det A = a_{11}\det A_{11} - a_{12}\det A_{12} + \cdots + (-1)^{1+n} a_{1n}\det A_{1n}
$$
where $A_{i,j}$ is the matrix obtained by eliminating row $i$ and column $j$ of $A$.

For example,
$$
\det \begin{pmatrix}
3 & -5 & 0 \\
2 & 4 & -1 \\
0 & 2 & 0
\end{pmatrix}
= 3 \begin{vmatrix}
4 & -1 \\
2 & 0
\end{vmatrix}
- (-5) \begin{vmatrix}
2 & -1 \\
0 & 0
\end{vmatrix}
+ 0 \begin{vmatrix}
2 & 4 \\
0 & 2
\end{vmatrix}
$$

### Cofactors and Triangular Matrices
The $(i, j)$ cofactor of an $n \times n$ matrix $A$ can be represented as follows.
$$C_{ij} = (-1)^{i+j} \det(A_{ij})$$
The determinant of a matrix $A$ can be computed down any row
or column of the matrix. For instance, down the $j$ th column,
the determinant can be shown as follows.
$$
\det A = a_{1j}C_{1j} + a_{2j}C_{2j} + \cdots + a_{nj}C_{nj}
$$
For example, Let's calculate following matrix $A$ using cofactors.
$$
A = \begin{pmatrix}
5 & 4 & 3 & 2 \\
0 & 1 & 2 & 0 \\
0 & -1 & 1 & 0 \\
0 & 1 & 1 & 3
\end{pmatrix} \\[5pt]

|A| = 5C_{11} + 0C_{21} + 0C_{31} + 0C_{41}\\[5pt] 
= 5(-1)^{1+1}
\begin{vmatrix}
1 & 2 & 0 \\
-1 & 1 & 0 \\
1 & 1 & 3
\end{vmatrix} \\[5pt]
= 5(0C_{13} + 0C_{23} + 3C_{33})
= 15(-1)^{3+3}
\begin{vmatrix}
1 & 2 \\
-1 & 1
\end{vmatrix} 
= 45
$$

### Determinant of Triangular Matrices
The determinant of Triangular matrices will be the product of
the entries on the main diagonal.  
If A is a triangular matrix then $\text{det} A = a_{11}a_{22} \cdots a_{nn}$

### Computational Eficiency
In general, the computation of a co-factor expansion for an $N \times N$ matrix requires roughly $N!$ multiplications. Co-factor expansions may not be practical, but determinants are still useful.  
Determinants are very useful in multivariable calculus for solving certain integration problems.
