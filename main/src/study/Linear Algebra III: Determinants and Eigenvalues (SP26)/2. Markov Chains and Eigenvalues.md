# Markov Chains and Eigenvalues
## Markov Chains
### Markov Chain Steady States
- A probability vector is a vector, $\vec{x}$, with non-negative elements that sum to 1.
- A stochastic matrix is a square matrix, $P$, whose columns are probability vectors.
- A Markov chain is a sequence of probability vectors $\vec{x_k}$, and a stochastic matrix $P$, such that: $\vec{x_{k+1}} = P \vec{x_k}; k = 0, 1, 2, \cdots$
- A steady-state vector for $P$ is a probability vector $\vec{q}$ such that $P \vec{q} = \vec{q}$.

### Markov Chain Convergence
#### Convergence
We often want to know what happens to a process, $\vec{x_{k+1}} = P \vec{x_k}; k = 0, 1, 2, \cdots$ as $k \rightarrow \infin$. If the sequence generated by will converge to a steady-state, and if so, what those steady-state vectors are.

#### Regular Stochastic Matrix
A stochastic matrix $P$ is regular if there is some $k$ such that $P^k$ only contains strictly positive entries.

#### Convergence and Regular Stochastic Matrix
If $P$ is a regular stochastic matrix, then $P$ has a unique steady-state vector $\vec{q}$ and $\vec{x_{k+1}} = P \vec{x_k}$ converges to $\vec{q}$ as $k \rightarrow \infin$.

## Eigenvalues and Eigenvectors
### Eigenvalues and Eigenvectors
If $A \in \mathbb{R^{n \times n}}$, and there is a $ \vec{v} \neq \vec{0}$ in $\mathbb{R^n}$,
$$
A \vec{v} = \lambda \vec{v}
$$
$\vec{v}$ is an eigenvector for $A$, and $\lambda \in \mathbb{C}$ is the corresponding eigenvalue.

Note that when all entries of $A$ and $\vec{v}$ are real, $\lambda$ can be complex (a rotation of the plane has no real eigenvalues. - 2차원에서 0,360이 아닌 회전은 방향 유지 안되기 때문에 실수 eigenvalue 없음. 실수 고유값이 존재하려면 어떤 벡터는 회전 후에도 같은 방향(또는 반대 방향)에 있어야 함)

- when $\lambda > 0$, $A \vec{v}$ and $\vec{v}$ point in the same direction.
- when $\lambda < 0$ $A \vec{v}$ and $\vec{v}$  point in opposite directions.

### Eigenspaces
Suppose $A \in \mathbb{R^{n \times n}}$, the eigenvectors for a given $\lambda$ span a
subspace of $\mathbb{R^n}$ called the $\lambda$-eigenspace of $A$.

$$
E_\lambda = \{\vec{v} \in \mathbb{R}^n \mid A\vec{v} = \lambda \vec{v}\}
$$
즉, eigenspace = 같은 eigenvalue $\lambda$를 가지는 모든 eigenvector들의 공간.

Also note that the  $\lambda$-eigenspace for matrix $A$ is Nul($A - \lambda I$).
$$
A\vec{v} = \lambda \vec{v} \\[5pt]
A\vec{v} - \lambda \vec{v} = 0 \\[5pt]
(A - \lambda I)\vec{v} = 0
$$
If $\vec{v} \neq 0$, we must have that ($A - \lambda I$) is singular. Thus, eigenvectors span the null space of ($A - \lambda I$).
	
### Eigenvalue Theorems
Theorems to identify eigenvalues and eigenvectors of a matrix.
- The diagonal elements of a triangular matrix are its eigenvalues.
- If matrix $A$ not invertible, $0$ is an eigenvalue of $A$.
- Stochastic matrices have an eigenvalue equal to 1.
- If $\vec{v_1}, \vec{v_2}, \cdots \vec{v_k}$ are eigenvectors that correspond to distinct eigenvalues, then $\vec{v_1}, \vec{v_2}, \cdots \vec{v_k}$ are linearly independent.
- Row reductions can change the eigenvalues of a matrix. Therefore, we cannot determine the eigenvalues of a matrix from its reduced form.
